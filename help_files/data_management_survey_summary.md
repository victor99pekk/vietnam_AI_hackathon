# Data Management for Training Large Language Models: A Survey
### Summary of Wang, Zhong, Wang, Zhu, Mi, Wang, Shang, Jiang & Liu (Peking University / Huawei Noah's Ark Lab), arXiv:2312.01700v3

---

## 1. Overview and Motivation

The authors define **data management** as the overall process of building a training dataset for LLMs: selecting data, combining it, deciding how to use it, and evaluating whether the chosen strategy worked. Their core observation is that although data is central to LLM success, most leading labs either don't disclose or only partially document how they built their training corpora, leaving practitioners without clear guidance.

The survey covers two stages of LLM development:
- **Pretraining** — building the large, diverse text corpus used for self-supervised learning.
- **Supervised fine-tuning (SFT)** — building the curated instruction datasets used to make models follow instructions and align with human expectations.

For each stage, the paper reviews the research literature on how different data-management choices affect model performance, and closes with a discussion of open challenges and future directions.

---

## 2. Pretraining Stage

### 2.1 Domain Composition

Pretraining corpora (e.g., the Pile) are typically mixtures of many source domains — web text, Wikipedia, books, dialogue, code, and academic text. The survey traces a historical trend: early models leaned heavily on high-diversity sources (web and Wiki); as the field began prioritizing quality, books and academic text were added; most recently, code and math data have taken a larger share, partly because research has shown code-heavy pretraining boosts reasoning ability.

Getting the **mixture ratio** right matters as much as including diverse domains. Early models relied on manual tuning and intuition. More recent techniques automate this:
- **Importance resampling** and **Group Distributionally Robust Optimization** to set domain weights automatically.
- **Gradient-based domain contribution** measurement to reweight sources.
- **Dynamic batch-level weighting** based on training loss.
- **"Data mixing laws"** that predict downstream model performance for a given mixture before training.

However, findings are not fully consistent: some studies suggest a diverse web-only mix can rival curated domain mixtures on certain tasks, and one study on code/natural-language mixing found that mixed-domain models perform close to, but not necessarily better than, domain-matched models under equal compute.

### 2.2 Data Quantity

#### 2.2.1 Scaling Laws
- **Kaplan et al. (2020)** established that test loss follows a power-law relationship with both model size and dataset size when neither is the bottleneck. Under a fixed compute budget, they found model size should grow faster than dataset size (roughly N ∝ C^0.73 vs D ∝ C^0.27).
- **Hoffmann et al. (2022)**, the "Chinchilla" paper, ran larger-scale experiments and derived a different empirical law, concluding that model size and data size should instead scale at roughly the same rate (N ∝ C^0.46, D ∝ C^0.54).
- **Su et al. (2024)** revisited Kaplan's methodology and refined the fitting procedure for the underlying constants.

#### 2.2.2 Data Repetition
Because high-quality data is finite, several works study what happens when the same data is trained on for multiple epochs:
- **Hernandez et al. (2022)** found repeated data can hurt performance, causing deviation from Kaplan's scaling law once models exceed ~100M parameters.
- **Muennighoff et al. (2023)** found that repeating a full dataset up to 4 epochs causes only minor harm relative to using equivalent unique data, and proposed a scaling law describing diminishing returns from repetition.
- **Xue et al. (2023)** documented a "multi-epoch degradation" phenomenon and identified dataset size, model size, and training objective as key drivers; standard regularization techniques mostly didn't help, except dropout.
- **Tirumala et al. (2023)** showed that repeating *carefully selected* data can outperform training on randomly chosen new data — i.e., smart repetition beats naive fresh sampling.
- A newer trend addresses data exhaustion by mixing in **synthetic data** generated by other models.

### 2.3 Data Quality

Quality control in pretraining datasets is typically organized as a pipeline: **quality filtering → deduplication → toxicity filtering**, with diversity and data recency treated as additional considerations.

#### 2.3.1 Quality Filtering
Common web-scraped corpora (e.g., Common Crawl) contain much low-quality content. Filtering approaches include hand-crafted heuristics, trained classifiers, metric-based thresholding (e.g., perplexity), and embedding-based cluster filtering. Filtering is generally shown to improve downstream performance, and several small, highly-filtered datasets have been used to train strong lightweight models. But there are caveats:
- Overly aggressive filtering can hurt performance broadly, because filtering proxies aren't perfectly representative of quality.
- A comparative study found perplexity-based pruning outperformed more complex approaches like memorization-based filtering.
- Another line of work built "data-centric" scaling laws showing that improving semantic and grammatical quality is a particularly effective lever.
- Overall, the field still lacks a theoretically grounded, universally accepted filtering strategy.

#### 2.3.2 Deduplication
Deduplication removes repeated documents/lines and is standard in most major pretraining pipelines. Benefits documented in the literature include reduced memorization, less train/test leakage, better training efficiency, and (per one study) a lower success rate for privacy-extraction attacks against the trained model.

Common techniques:
- **N-gram and hashing methods** (the most widely used), applied at line-level, document-level, or both.
- **Neural-model-based dedup**, shown in one study to outperform traditional n-gram/hashing approaches.
- **Semantic deduplication (SemDeDup)**, which removes near-duplicate content by clustering in embedding space rather than relying on surface text overlap.

#### 2.3.3 Toxicity Filtering
Toxicity filtering removes disrespectful or harmful language from training corpora, typically using heuristic/rule-based filters or trained n-gram/neural classifiers. While effective at reducing toxic generations, the survey highlights a fairness trade-off: aggressive toxicity filtering has been shown to reduce a model's general capability and its own ability to *recognize* toxicity, and can disproportionately suppress text associated with minority dialects and identities — raising bias concerns.

#### 2.3.4 Data Diversity
- One study measured the format diversity of public pretraining datasets using a "Task2Vec diversity coefficient" and found it to be high.
- **D2 Pruning** was proposed to jointly balance diversity and difficulty during data selection, representing the dataset as a graph and using forward/reverse message passing to select a subgraph that is both diverse and appropriately difficult.

#### 2.3.5 Data Age
Because some knowledge is time-sensitive, more recent models tend to use more recent data. Research found that a temporal mismatch between the evaluation period and the pretraining data period can distort measured performance, and that this misalignment isn't easily fixed by later fine-tuning — especially for larger models.

### 2.4 Interactions Between Domain, Quantity, and Quality
Newer scaling laws try to capture how these factors interact jointly rather than in isolation: bivariate laws predicting performance from both data quantity and domain ratio; laws describing the quality/quantity trade-off under fixed compute; and findings that data quality's benefit becomes more pronounced at larger model scale (with fixed data quantity). Some researchers also stress the importance of **global deduplication across domains** (not just within a single source), and note that domains combining high quality with high diversity tend to be most beneficial.

## 3. Supervised Fine-Tuning (SFT) Stage

### 3.1 Task Composition
Multitask fine-tuning — training on a wide range of task types — reliably improves generalization to unseen tasks across model scales from 3B to 540B parameters. As the number of tasks scales up, the *mixture ratio* across tasks becomes important and is usually still set by experimentation/intuition rather than a principled method. Some approaches select relevant task subsets automatically using representation similarity or gradient similarity to a target task.

There's an important tension here: mixing tasks can help when data volume is modest but **hurt performance when the mixed data volume grows too large**, possibly due to conflicting data formats/distributions across tasks. Different instruction datasets also tend to instill different specific capabilities, so no single dataset or fixed combination dominates across all evaluations. An alternative approach — training many single-task expert models and combining them, rather than one multitask model — has shown promise but is more resource-intensive.

### 3.2 Data Quality

#### 3.2.1 Instruction Quality
Instruction data quality is widely regarded as one of the biggest levers for SFT performance. Filtering methods include:
- Heuristic/model-based signals like perplexity and uncertainty.
- Loss-based and output-probability-based scoring for more complex quality metrics.
- Search-based methods (e.g., BlendSearch) to locate high-quality instructions meeting certain criteria.
- Using LLMs themselves as **quality judges** — either self-evaluating their own outputs iteratively, or using a stronger model (e.g., ChatGPT) or a reward model to score/filter data.
- A newer "weak-to-strong" strategy uses smaller, weaker models to select high-quality data for training larger ones.

#### 3.2.2 Instruction Diversity
Diversity of instructions (in intent and semantics) also improves performance and robustness, but there's no universally agreed way to measure it quantitatively. Notable approaches:
- **#InsTag**: uses ChatGPT-generated fine-grained tags and measures diversity as the coverage rate of unique tags.
- **Rouge-L similarity**, **embedding distance**, and **scoring models** are used to select instructions that differ from each other at the literal, semantic, and human-alignment levels.
- For narrow, domain-specific tasks where diversity is naturally constrained, some works use LLM-driven **active search** to generate more varied instruction variations.

#### 3.2.3 Instruction Complexity
Complex instructions are important for developing strong instruction-following and reasoning ability.
- **#InsTag** also quantifies complexity as the average number of tags per instruction.
- Another approach evaluates complexity along eight dimensions covering the length, content, and format of inputs and task descriptions.
- To increase complexity, some methods incrementally augment instructions — adding nodes to a semantic tree, or applying operations like adding constraints, deepening reasoning requirements, or "in-breadth" evolution of the instruction (as in Evol-Instruct-style methods).

### 3.3 Data Quantity
Unlike the relatively consistent pretraining scaling laws, findings on instruction data quantity diverge:
- Earlier work (following the pretraining precedent) argued more instruction data is generally better.
- More recent work argues that **quality matters more than quantity**, and that a small, carefully curated dataset (on the order of ~1,000 examples in some cases) can be highly effective (e.g., the LIMA "less is more" finding).
- A newer power-based multiplicative scaling law shows that scaling up fine-tuning data further can still improve performance even after strong results are reached with limited data — suggesting no hard ceiling on the benefit of more data.
- Several papers find that **different capabilities scale differently**: general instruction-following ability plateaus quickly (after roughly 1,000 examples in one study), while mathematical reasoning and code generation continue to improve steadily with more data. Findings on this differ across studies, however — one large empirical study on real-world use cases found the opposite pattern, and another found abilities scale in qualitatively different ways from each other.

### 3.4 Dynamic Data-Efficient Learning
Rather than treating the instruction dataset as static, some approaches integrate data selection directly into the fine-tuning loop:

**Training affects data (dynamic pruning during training):**
- Periodic filtering of "unimportant" examples during SFT using extended EL2N-style importance metrics.
- Classifying model responses as "answer-like or not" to gauge instruction-following ability and serve as an early-stopping signal.
- Active task search that selects informative tasks based on prompt uncertainty, in a training loop.

**Data affects training (adapting the training strategy instead):**
- Treating task selection as a continual-learning "replay" strategy to reduce forgetting and negative task interference.
- Sequential learning of specialized then general abilities while retaining a small proportion of specialized data (the DMT approach).
- Treating different data sources as coarse reward signals for mixed-quality LLM-generated data (the OpenChat/C-RLFT approach).
- Curriculum-style approaches that progress the model from easier to harder examples, based on data quality, instruction complexity, or task difficulty.

### 3.5 Interactions Between Task, Quality, and Quantity
Analysis using #InsTag shows that larger open-source SFT datasets tend to also be more diverse and to yield better performance. Most current data-selection work jointly considers quality and diversity together rather than either alone. Because different capabilities scale at different rates (Section 3.3), the authors argue more sophisticated task-composition strategies are still needed to train strong multi-task models.

## 4. Challenges and Future Directions

The authors identify several open problems:

1. **General data management framework** — There's no broadly accepted, unified framework for building datasets across use cases; more autonomous systems (potentially using LLMs as quality evaluators or data augmentors) are needed to reduce the manual effort currently required.
2. **Debiasing and detoxifying data** — As LLMs are deployed more broadly, addressing embedded social bias and toxicity in training data (without introducing new distortions) becomes increasingly critical.
3. **Multimodal data management** — Most current research is text-only; extending data-management principles to vision, audio, and other modalities is an open area.
4. **Data management for self-exploration** — Supporting LLMs that actively explore environments/tasks will require new data pipelines suited to large-scale interaction data.
5. **Efficient filtering of synthesized data** — As human-annotated data becomes exhausted, LLM-generated synthetic data is increasingly used, but ensuring its quality via filtering remains underdeveloped.
6. **Fine-grained data ordering** — Emerging evidence suggests the *order* in which data is presented (in both pretraining and SFT) affects performance and deserves more study.
7. **Conflicted data separation** — In multi-task fine-tuning, data conflicts between tasks can hurt performance; better methods are needed to detect and handle such conflicts.

---

## 5. Conclusion

The survey provides a structured review of data management practices across both the pretraining and SFT stages of LLM development, organizing existing research by aspect (domain/task composition, quantity, quality, and — for SFT — dynamic learning strategies). It closes by highlighting open challenges intended to guide future research and practical dataset-building efforts.

## Appendix A: Key Takeaways (as stated by the authors)

**Pretraining:**
- Broad domain coverage and a well-tuned mixture ratio both matter; automatic mixture-weighting methods are improving but still have room to grow.
- Large data volume is broadly considered important; modest, well-chosen repetition can also help.
- Quality control typically follows the order: quality filtering → deduplication → toxicity filtering — though overly aggressive filtering (quality or toxicity) can degrade performance or introduce social bias, an area still under-explored.
- Data diversity and temporal alignment with evaluation data both affect performance and warrant further study.

**SFT:**
- Multitask fine-tuning is now standard, but task conflicts remain a problem; ensembling single-task experts is an emerging alternative to one multitask model.
- Quality control relies on heuristics, human evaluation, or LLM-as-judge approaches; instruction diversity and complexity both help, but how to systematically maximize them remains open.
- SFT performance tends to depend more on data quality than sheer quantity, though data requirements vary substantially by task/capability.
- Rather than keeping the instruction set fixed, dynamically adjusting data during fine-tuning (and designing training strategies around the data) is an active and growing direction.

## Appendix B: Additional Aspects of Data Management

### B.1 Social Bias
Pretrained LLMs absorb social biases present in their training text. Specific findings cited:
- Quality filters similar to those used for GPT-3 were found (via analysis of a U.S. high-school newspaper dataset) to favor newspapers from larger, wealthier, more urban, more educated school districts — a form of language ideology bias baked into "quality" filtering.
- A case study on media political bias in pretraining data showed it propagates through to downstream fairness in hate-speech and misinformation detection tasks, correlated with partisan lean.
- Recommendations include documenting social biases, representational harms, and excluded voices when releasing large web-text corpora.
- Open questions remain around mitigating pretraining bias, understanding bias in SFT data specifically, and whether SFT itself can help reduce inherited social bias.

### B.2 Prompt Design
How instructions/prompts are phrased affects performance:
- Prompts are either hand-written or synthetically generated by strong models, and the specific phrasing chosen can cause significant performance swings.
- Reformulating prompts into more model-friendly forms, and choosing the lowest-perplexity phrasing among alternatives, both improve results.
- A framework (UIT) was developed to automatically unify instruction formats across different datasets.
- Surprising findings: continuous-prompt interpretations don't always match their discrete-prompt counterparts as expected; removing task-definition components — keeping only output-space/label information — barely hurts performance, suggesting models may rely on fairly superficial cues from instructions; comparable performance can even be achieved using deliberately incorrect input-output examples paired with output-space info, casting doubt on how much genuine task understanding fine-tuning actually instills.
- On the source of prompts: fine-tuning a weaker model on a stronger model's outputs tends to make it imitate the stronger model's *style* without truly gaining its *capability*. Relatedly, human-authored data has been shown to outperform GPT-4-generated synthetic data by a notable margin in at least one comparison.

### B.3 Hallucinations
LLM hallucination (generating content that conflicts with the input, context, or facts) is linked in several studies to gaps or errors in the knowledge absorbed during pretraining. Mitigation strategies discussed:
- Careful curation of pretraining corpora toward higher-quality data (used by GPT-3, Llama 2, Falcon, among others).
- Both manually curated and automatically selected high-quality SFT instruction data have been shown experimentally to reduce hallucination.
- Overall, the authors frame data management (at both stages) as a promising lever for hallucination mitigation.

## Appendix C: Related Surveys (as discussed by the authors)
The paper distinguishes itself from several adjacent surveys:
- A broad LLM development survey covering many topics beyond data.
- A survey covering the LLM evolution from model, data, and downstream-task angles.
- A general data-centric-AI survey not specific to LLMs.
- A survey specifically on instruction tuning methodology and data construction.
- A survey on aligning LLMs with human expectations, covering data collection, training methods, and evaluation.

This paper differentiates itself by focusing specifically and systematically on data management across *both* the pretraining and SFT stages together, with attention to strategy evaluation and practical guidance.
