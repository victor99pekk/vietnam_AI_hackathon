Alan Turing was born in London, England in 1912. He studied at King's College, Cambridge and later earned his PhD from Princeton University.

During World War II, Turing worked at Bletchley Park, where he played a crucial role in breaking the German Enigma code. He developed the Bombe, an electromechanical device used to decipher Enigma-encrypted messages.

After the war, Turing worked at the National Physical Laboratory and later at the University of Manchester. He is widely considered the father of theoretical computer science and artificial intelligence. His 1950 paper "Computing Machinery and Intelligence" introduced the Turing Test.

Turing also made significant contributions to mathematical biology, publishing work on morphogenesis. He died in 1954 in Wilmslow, England.
